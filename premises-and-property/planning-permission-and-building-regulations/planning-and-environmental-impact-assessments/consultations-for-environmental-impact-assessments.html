Planning and Environmental Impact Assessments

Consultations for Environmental Impact Assessments

If you are planning a Schedule 1 development, or a Schedule 2 project which requires an Environmental Impact Assessment , you will need to be involved in statutory consultations. It is also advisable to carry out some informal, voluntary consultations before you get to the stage of statutory consultations.

Preliminary consultations

You will probably find it useful to talk informally with your local planning authority (LPA) at an early stage of your planning process. This will help you to clarify the likely environmental effects of your development proposal and find out how the LPA expects you to reduce and repair them. See the pages in this guide on developments which need an Environmental Impact Assessment and when you need an environmental statement and what it should cover. You may also find it useful at this stage to talk to other organisations with statutory environmental responsibilities, such as English Nature or the Environment Agency.

You can find LPA contact details on the Directgov website - Opens in a new window.

Other consultations

While you are preparing the environmental statement (ES), you can benefit from consulting public and non-statutory bodies that are concerned with environmental issues. These consultations will allow you to find out what these groups think about the effects of your development, and work out how to make it more sustainable.

Statutory and public consultations by the LPA

Before deciding on a Schedule 1 or Schedule 2 planning application, your LPA should consult public bodies that have statutory environmental responsibilities. In England, these are:

  • the principal council for the area where the proposed development will be situated, if this is not the LPA
  • the Countryside Commission and English Nature
  • the Environment Agency
  • statutory consultees for the planning application for the proposed development, such as the Regional Development Agency, Health & Safety Executive, local highway authority

These statutory consultation bodies are required to provide you with any information they possess which you need to prepare your ES. You may have to pay a fee to cover their costs in providing the information. See the page in this guide on preparing and submitting an environmental statement. LPAs should allow the public to comment on your ES and planning application, and should take their views into account when deciding on your planning application.

If you submit your ES with the planning application, the LPA will be responsible for advertising it. They will do this by placing a notice in a local newspaper and also at or near the proposed development site. If you submit the ES after your planning application, you are responsible for publicising it. Either way, the LPA will place your planning application and ES on the planning register, where the public will be able to review and comment on them. See the page in this guide on when you need an environmental statement and what it should cover.

LPAs also have to forward the ES and planning application to the Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government.
