Enabling the Transition to a Green Economy - a resource-smart economy

Resource efficiency in a green economy

The efficient use of natural resources is important both for the environment - for example, by reducing the emissions and biodiversity impacts associated with extraction, and for increasing the competitiveness of UK businesses.

Most businesses have made significant gains in resource efficiency, driven by competition and the need to lower costs.

However, recent government research shows there could be considerable potential for savings of around £23 billion per year from simple measures, of which £18 billion are associated with using raw materials more efficiently and reducing waste. A further £33 billion per year might be achievable from more expensive measures, ie with over one year payback. These savings are spread across the economy.

Download the Further Benefits of Business Resource Efficiency report from the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) website (PDF, 3.05MB) - Opens in a new window 

Resource efficiency is also key to addressing resource security risks.

UK businesses rely on a predictable supply of resources. But population growth and higher rates of consumption are likely to increase demand in the future. This will put additional pressure on supplies of limited natural resources and services and could lead to recurring interruptions to supply and price spikes.

Reducing reliance on critical resources through improved efficiency, as well as greater reuse, remanufacturing and recycling, will:

  • build resilience to price and supply fluctuations
  • lessen environmental impacts by reducing demand for primary production

The waste and energy efficiency policy frameworks will be important drivers of reduced resource use.

The government will also continue to raise awareness and enable businesses to improve their resource efficiency. They will also work internationally - for example, through relevant European Union (EU) forums - to help ensure open access to globally-traded resources.

Specifically, the government will:

  • continue to provide advice and information to businesses on reducing resource use
  • work with businesses to assess and provide information on resource risks
  • produce an 'Action Plan on Resource Security' by February 2012 to assist business with strategic risk management and recovery of critical resources
  • work with the EU to liberalise trade in key materials, and to avoid protectionist measures such as export quotas and stockpiling
